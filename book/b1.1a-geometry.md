# B1.1a Geometry

## Problem-Based Scenario: The Distorted Distance

**The Challenge:** You are processing a multibeam echo sounder survey conducted near the equator. Your software computes a specific distance between two seabed features, but an independent survey using different tools provides a slightly different distance. 

Why do these measurements diverge over long distances, and how does the Earth's true shape affect spatial computations? The answer requires understanding the complex geometry of conic sections, specifically the reference ellipsoid, and how we map features using parametric equations.

## (i) Conic Sections, Geometry of the Ellipse and of the Ellipsoid

### Foundations of Conic Sections

Conic sections are mathematical curves generated by the intersection of a flat plane with the surface of a double-napped right circular cone. Depending on the precise angle of the intersecting plane relative to the central longitudinal axis, four distinct families of curves emerge:

1. **Circle:** The cutting plane is parallel to the circular base.
2. **Ellipse:** The intersecting plane cuts entirely through one nappe at a shallow angle.
3. **Parabola:** The intersecting plane is strictly parallel to the generator line.
4. **Hyperbola:** The plane is oriented at a steep angle, intersecting both halves of the double cone.

The fundamental shape is dictated by **eccentricity** ($e$).
- Circle: $e = 0$
- Ellipse: $0 < e < 1$
- Parabola: $e = 1$
- Hyperbola: $e > 1$

### The Ellipse and Eccentricity

In hydrography, the ellipse is the most critical conic section. The Earth assumes the shape of an oblate spheroid, or an ellipsoid of revolution, due to its rotation.

Let's define an ellipse using its semi-major axis ($a$) and semi-minor axis ($b$). The **first eccentricity** ($e$) is defined as:

$$ e^2 = rac{a^2 - b^2}{a^2} $$

The **flattening** ($f$) is another common parameter:

$$ f = rac{a - b}{a} $$

### The Reference Ellipsoid in Hydrography

To map the seabed accurately, hydrographers operate within the constraints of a reference ellipsoid (like WGS84). 

For WGS84:
- Semi-major axis ($a$): 6378137.0 m
- Flattening ($f$): 1 / 298.257223563

## (ii) Parametric Equations of Curves and Surfaces

### 2D Parametric Curves

Parametric equations express a set of quantities as explicit functions of independent variables (parameters). For a 2D curve like an ellipse:

$$ x(t) = a \cdot \cos(t) $$
$$ y(t) = b \cdot \sin(t) $$

where $t$ is the parameter (often angle or time).

### 3D Parametric Surfaces

To model 3D structures like the reference ellipsoid, two independent parameters (e.g., latitude $\phi$ and longitude $\lambda$) are required. 

The Cartesian coordinates $(X, Y, Z)$ are expressed parametrically as:

$$ X(\phi, \lambda) = N(\phi) \cdot \cos(\phi) \cdot \cos(\lambda) $$
$$ Y(\phi, \lambda) = N(\phi) \cdot \cos(\phi) \cdot \sin(\lambda) $$
$$ Z(\phi, \lambda) = N(\phi) \cdot (1 - e^2) \cdot \sin(\phi) $$

Where $N(\phi)$ is the prime vertical radius of curvature.

---

**Glossary**
* **Eccentricity:** A dimensionless parameter describing how much a conic section deviates from a perfect circle.
* **Oblate Spheroid:** A sphere flattened at the poles, representing the shape of the Earth.
* **Parametric Equation:** An equation where coordinates are expressed as functions of one or more independent parameters.
