# F1.1 Physical Geodesy

## Problem-Based Scenario: The "Uphill" Flow
**The Challenge:** You are conducting a hydrographic survey for a new canal system designed to connect two large inland lakes. Using highly precise GPS, you establish the geometric heights of the water level at both ends of the proposed canal. The GPS indicates that Lake A is exactly 2 meters higher than Lake B. However, when the canal is opened, water begins to flow from Lake B into Lake A. 

How is it possible that water flows "uphill" according to your GPS? 

The answer lies in Physical Geodesy. Water responds to the physical force of gravity (potential energy), not purely geometric distances from the center of the Earth. To understand this phenomenon, we must explore the Earth's gravity field, equipotential surfaces, and how we define "height".

## F1.1a Gravity field of the Earth

### (i) Equipotential surfaces

The Earth's gravity field is not uniform; it varies due to the planet's rotation, equatorial bulging (oblateness), and uneven distribution of mass (like dense mountain ranges or deep ocean trenches). 

An **equipotential surface** (or level surface) is a surface on which the gravitational potential energy is exactly the same everywhere. Because the potential is constant, there is no gravitational force acting along the surface itself. Water at rest will perfectly align itself with an equipotential surface. The most important equipotential surface in geodesy is the **[Geoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoid)**, which roughly coincides with global Mean Sea Level (MSL) extended continuously through the continents.

### (ii) Gravity and gravity anomalies

If the Earth were a perfectly uniform, rotating ellipsoid, gravity would increase smoothly from the equator to the poles. This theoretical, mathematically perfect model is called **Normal Gravity** ($\gamma$). We use the [Somigliana equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_gravity#Somigliana_equation) (based on the GRS80 or WGS84 ellipsoid) to calculate this.

However, the actual, physical gravity ($g$) measured on the surface differs from Normal Gravity due to local mass concentrations. The difference between actual observed gravity and the theoretical normal gravity is known as a **[Gravity Anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_anomaly)** ($\Delta g = g - \gamma$). 

Hydrographers and geophysicists use corrections like the **Free-Air Anomaly** (correcting for elevation above the ellipsoid without accounting for terrain mass) and the **Bouguer Anomaly** (which removes the gravitational pull of the rock mass between the station and the datum) to understand sub-surface geology and define the Geoid.

**Python Snippet: Calculating Normal Gravity**
```python
import math

def calculate_normal_gravity(latitude_degrees: float) -> float:
    """
    Calculates the normal gravity (gamma) on the WGS84 ellipsoid
    using the Somigliana equation.
    
    Returns:
        Normal gravity in m/s^2
    """
    # WGS84 Constants
    gamma_e = 9.7803253359  # Normal gravity at equator (m/s^2)
    gamma_p = 9.8321849378  # Normal gravity at pole (m/s^2)
    a = 6378137.0           # Semi-major axis (m)
    b = 6356752.3142        # Semi-minor axis (m)
    
    # Convert latitude to radians
    lat_rad = math.radians(latitude_degrees)
    sin_lat = math.sin(lat_rad)
    cos_lat = math.cos(lat_rad)
    
    # Somigliana equation
    num = (a * gamma_e * (cos_lat**2)) + (b * gamma_p * (sin_lat**2))
    den = math.sqrt((a**2 * (cos_lat**2)) + (b**2 * (sin_lat**2)))
    
    return num / den

# Example: Gravity at 45 degrees North
# print(calculate_normal_gravity(45.0)) # ~9.806 m/s^2
```

## F1.1b Global geopotential models

### (i) Spherical harmonics

To map the irregular Geoid across the entire globe, geodesists use **Global Geopotential Models (GGMs)**. Because the gravity potential field behaves as a harmonic function in the free space outside the Earth, it can be mathematically modeled using a series expansion called **[Spherical Harmonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_harmonics)**.

Think of spherical harmonics as a three-dimensional Fourier transform. Just as a complex sound wave can be broken down into simple sine and cosine waves of different frequencies, the Earth's lumpy gravity field can be broken down into a series of spherical shapes (harmonics) of increasing complexity (degree and order). 
- Low-degree harmonics describe the broad, global shape (like the equatorial bulge).
- High-degree harmonics describe localized, high-frequency changes (like mountain ranges).

Modern models like EGM2008 extend to degree and order 2159, providing a global spatial resolution of roughly 9 kilometers.
